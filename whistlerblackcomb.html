Snowpack Description

The surface consists of recent 10-30 cm hard slab snow layers above 2100 m. From the surface to 2100 m, the snowpack is moist to wet, with variable snow depths ranging from 60 cm in mid-elevations to over 1 meter in alpine zones. Near the base lies a persistent melt-freeze crust from November 25th, with a thin ice crust layer also present above it in some areas. Moist layers underlie the recent slabs. This basal crust should be continually monitored.

Closing Summary

This report highlights a moderate Alpine avalanche hazard driven by a thin storm slab layer and variable snowpack conditions. Although no significant avalanche events have been observed, careful route selection and continuous monitoring are essential—especially above 2200m. Backcountry travellers should prioritize updated avalanche bulletins, favor terrain with stable snow conditions, and remain vigilant to any signs of rapid change. Emphasis should be placed on managing exposure in moist snowpack areas below 2100m while exploiting the more defined hard slab conditions in the alpine zones for safer travel.

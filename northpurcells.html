Previous Reports

Previous operations involved managed travel on simple avalanche terrain with emphasis on careful terrain selection. Guides operated under conditions of low-angle (<30°) slopes, adapting to wind influences, marginal bonding near the melt-freeze crust, and isolated areas of wind slab. These observations underscored the importance of thorough stability assessments and consistent risk management.

Key Actions & Safety Considerations

  • Avoid areas with active storm slab signatures, particularly where the melt-freeze crust is present.
  • Select routes that minimize exposure to steep, convex slopes where slab propagation is possible.
  • Continuously monitor wind-loaded areas and verify terrain stability before committing.
  • Maintain vigilance for signs of instability (cracking, sluffing) and adjust travel plans accordingly.
  • Use conservative decision-making given moderate confidence levels and variable alpine observations.

Closing Summary

This report highlights significant avalanche activity dominated by natural size 3 storm slab events, supported by new avalanche detections and complex snowpack layering from a recent snowfall over an existing melt-freeze crust. Operators are advised to seek well-assessed terrain with minimal steep slopes and avoid areas of widespread storm slab development. Emphasize continual assessment of wind influences and weak layer propagation. Safe backcountry travel will depend on strategic route selection, up-to-date stability checks, and adherence to risk management protocols in these conditions.
